What this study is about

We revisited 163 permanent vegetation plots across Iceland
— some after just a few years, others after more than two decades —
and asked a simple question:


Has the plant life changed?

The short answer is: yes, and in ways that are consistent enough to measure.

The dataset at a glance
Plots revisited 163
Total species records 5374
Unique plant taxa 226
Shortest gap between surveys 9 years
Longest gap between surveys 25 years
Median gap 12 years

Each plot is compared to itself — first survey vs. last survey.
This removes the noise of plots being in different places or climates.

Finding 1 — Species richness has shifted

Most plots did not stay the same.

Points above the line = more species now. Points below = fewer.

Finding 1 — How much did richness change?

34% of plots gained species  ·  60% lost species  ·  median change: -2

Finding 2 — Diversity (not just count) also changed

Species richness only counts how many species are present.
Shannon diversity also weighs how evenly they share the space.

Median Shannon change: -0.397  ·  Median evenness change: -0.111

Finding 3 — Total plant cover changed too

Not just which species are there — but how much vegetation there is overall.

Points above the line = more plant cover now.

Finding 4 — The dominant species are different now

The plants that dominated a plot in the first survey are often not the same ones dominating in the last.

Finding 5 — The composition of whole communities shifted

Bray-Curtis dissimilarity measures how different a plot’s species list is between surveys.
0 = identical. 1 = nothing in common.

A median of 0.61 means the typical plot has changed substantially.

Finding 6 — Community change is statistically real

We tested whether the difference between first and last surveys is larger than
what we’d expect by chance alone, while controlling for each plot’s identity.


Test R² F p-value
Paired PERMANOVA (999 permutations) 0.0273 8.99 0.001


Plain language: The first-vs-last difference is real, not random noise.
It’s small (R² = 2.7%) but highly consistent across plots.

Finding 8 — Specific species are associated with change

IndVal analysis identifies taxa that are consistently more common in

What is driving the change?

We cannot say definitively from this dataset alone — but the patterns are consistent with:

  • Vegetation becoming taller and denser in many plots
    (vascular cover and height are the strongest predictors of community pattern)

  • Soil type and moisture matter — some habitat types changed more than others

  • The coastal sand plots are in their own world — dune/beach communities
    shifted so dramatically they sit completely apart from the rest of the dataset


These findings are correlational. A designed resurvey with climate and land-use
data alongside would let us test why communities are shifting.

Bottom line: Icelandic vegetation plots have changed between surveys in
ways that are consistent, measurable, and not explained by random variation.

Next steps


Short term — squeeze more from this dataset

  • Add survey-gap as a covariate: does more time = more change?
  • Split analysis by soil type to ask which habitats are most vulnerable
  • Spatial analysis: is change clustered geographically?


Longer term — if new fieldwork is possible

  • Resurvey a subset of plots with standardised protocol
  • Collect paired climate / land-use covariates
  • Move from “did it change?” to “why did it change?”

Methods in brief

Step Detail
Data v_plants_allt.csv · 163 revisited plots
Cover harmonisation Braun-Blanquet codes → midpoints; numeric values used directly
Paired design Each plot compared to itself (first vs last survey only)
Diversity Species richness S, Shannon H, Simpson D, Pielou J
Composition Bray-Curtis dissimilarity, NMDS (k=2), paired PERMANOVA
Environmental fit vegan::envfit on strict driver set
Indicator taxa indicspecies::multipatt IndVal.g, BH-adjusted FDR
Outliers RN-02, RN-03, RN-65-01 excluded from NMDS (coastal dune plots)
Software R · tidyverse · vegan · indicspecies
