Abstract

Introduction
Excessive organic enrichment and hHypoxic conditions in aquatic systems are a growing problem worldwide, and such events can occur at seasonal, local, and even global and geological scales (Rabalais et al. 2001, 2010; Diaz and Rosenberg 2008; Levin et al. 2009; Zhang et al. 2010; Caswell and Frid 2017; Alosairi et al. 2021). Hypoxic Such conditions   commonly arise because offrom anthropogenic eutrophication (Malone and Newton 2020), where in which an ecosystem becomes overloadedflowed with nutrient stressors (sewage, waste, fertiliszers, or other organic material). The subsequent usage of nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus by macrophytes, phytoplankton, and bacteria in biological processes may require more oxygen than the system can supply, leading to hypoxic (or anoxic) conditions that are lethal or sub-lethal to pelagic and benthic organisms. Ecosystem recovery can take years, even decades or longer (<U+00C6>rtebjerg et al. 2003; Diaz and Rosenberg 2008; Jones and Schmitz 2009; Borja et al. 2010, Malone and Newton 2020; Le Moal, Gascuel-Odoux et al. 2019).
There are three main types of hypoxic organic enrichment conditions: seasonal, episodic (e.g. following flooding) and permanent (Levin et al. 2009). Hypoxic conditions commonly occur when water columns become stratified, which prevents oxygenated surface waters from mixing with lower-oxygen water near the bottom. Various taxa display differing thresholds for tolerance of low oxygen conditions (Vaquer-Sunyer and Duarte 2008; Belley et al. 2010) but such responses also depend on the size of the affected area<U+2019>s size and exposure duration of exposure (Levin et al. 2009). Wijnhoven et al. (2010) defined hypoxia (low oxygen) as O2<3 mg/l but and anoxia (no oxygen) ats O2<0.5 mg/l, whereas Rabalais et al. (2010) defined hypoxic conditions as O2<2 mg/l. Vaquer-Sunyer and Duarte (2008) found that the standard common definition of 2 mg O2/l as hypoxic was below empirical sub-lethal and lethal O2 thresholds for 50% of the species tested, which implyingied  that past and present distributions of hypoxic impacts on marine life have generally have been underestimated.
Eutrophication and hHypoxic conditions can be permanent (non-reversible), which makes it difficult for benthic animals to re-establish (Wijnhoven et al. 2010; Belley et al. 2010; Raman et al. 2015) but can also occur seasonally in water bodies that are largely land-locked or have narrow outlets relative to their volume. Examples include estuaries, fjords, lagoons, or even land-locked oceans (e.g. Baltic and Black Seas) which have relatively limited water exchange with sources of oxygenated water and relatively long residence times (Levin et al. 2009). To date, many of the fjord-like hypoxic ecosystems studied so far with respect to benthic fauna are estuaries with seasonal hypoxia, i.e. transition zones where freshwater from land and seawater mix with the occasional hyperabundance of fresh or saline conditions leading to stratifications (with seasonal nutrient or salinity overflows) and subsequent oxygen depletion, but also periods of recovery in between the seasonal stress (Leon-Morales and Vargas 1998; Como and Magni 2009; Yoshino et al. 2010; D<U+00ED>az-Asencio et al. 2015). Studies of one-time hypoxic events (one-time hypoxic conditions with subsequent recovery for years without repeated hypoxic stressors), are relatively rare for marine/saline fjords, especially in the Northern Hemisphere (Micaroni et al. 2021). They are commonly associated with anthropogenic eutrophication although such events might also arise due to natural phases of seasonal cycles (Josefson and Widbom 1988; Fallesen et al. 2000; Nilsson and Rosenberg 2000; Hansen et al. 2002).
Ecosystem recovery after excessive organic enrichment is a complex process influenced by a suite of biotic and abiotic factors. Key determinants include the magnitude and duration of enrichment (Pearson and Rosenberg 1978; Keeley at al. 2015), sediment characteristics and hydrodynamics (Hargrave et al. 2008; Martinez-Garcia et al. 2015), background community structure and functional diversity (Villn<U+00E4>s et al. 2011; Loreau et al. 2001), oxygen availability (Hargrave et al. 2008; Gray et al. 2002), and management interventions like fallowing or remediation (Macleod et al. 2006; Brooks et al. 2003). The interplay between all factors shapes the trajectory and timescale of recovery, with some systems demonstrating rapid functional restoration while others exhibit prolonged or incomplete recovery (Steckbauer et al. 2011). Understanding these drivers is essential for effective management and restoration of impacted marine benthic habitats.
In the winter of 2012<U+2013>2013, a massive hypoxic and organic enrichment event occurred in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur fjord, West-Iceland (Fig. 1). Approximately 300 thousand tonnes (~70% of the Icelandic spawning stock) of herring (Clupea harengus) entered the semi-enclosed 6 km long fjord (~10 km2) as part of their natural overwintering behaviour (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018; J<U+00F3>nsson et al. 2019). A combination of oxygen consumption by the colossal quantity of herring in the 0.2 km3 fjord (this amount of herring in the fjord might lower the oxygen concentrations to hypoxic levels in a few hours (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018)), unusually calm and cold weather and surface ice formation limiting oxygen exchange with the air, along with the semi-enclosed nature of the fjord, caused a mass fish kill of a cumulative total 55 thousand tonnes of herring in two separate occasions six weeks apart (hereafter herring event). The full volume of dead herring in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur in winter 2012<U+2013>2013 equated to 180 million individuals and was of similar magnitude as Iceland<U+2019>s entire allowable herring catch in the 2012<U+2013>2013 fishing season (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018; J<U+00F3>nsson et al. 2019). Oxygen saturation levels in the fjord in the days following the mass fish kill incidents were as low as 1.1 ml/L near the bottom (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018).
The magnitude of the fish death was clearly visible, as approximately 7 thousand tons of herring washed ashore during the later incident (Fig. 2). The immediate consequences for the benthic fauna were less clear. However, although numerous dead invertebrates of diverse taxa also washed ashore along with the dead herring (Stef<U+00E1>nsson and von Schmalensee 2013). Given the low oxygen levels near the bottom of the fjord during the hypoxic conditions that led to the fish kills (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018), it is likely that the benthic fauna was also influenced at that time. In the weeks and months that followed, the benthic ecosystem experienced additional environmental stress, as thousands of tons of decomposing herring caused extensive organic enrichment on the bottom and probably further hypoxia in the benthic sediments.
The herring event and the following organic enrichment of the bottom of Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur in the winter 2012<U+2013>2013, was were an isolated incident, as no further fish kills have occurred there. The herring death thus presented a unique opportunity to study the effects of such an event on the marine benthic community follow and the recovery of the benthic ecosystem in the years after the event. Mass fish kills are increasing worldwide, with numerous negative consequences (La and Cooke 2011; Alosairi et al. 2021). Understanding the effects such events can have on other species is therefore of the utmost importance. 
This study began immediately in the first summer after the herring deaths and yearly sampling ran for six five years from 2013<U+2013>2017. The aim was twofold: 1) Estimate the effects of the organic enrichment on the benthos of Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur, by comparing our data from 2013<U+2013>2017 to a dataset from 1999 using the same sampling stations from 1999 (Ing<U+00F3>lfsson 1999), while also exploring inter-annual variations within the 2013<U+2013>2017 period; 2) Quantify how species composition and number of individual macrofauna recovered after the environmental shock event and estimate the amount of time needed until full recovery. Specifically, we looked at the emergence of species capable of decomposing the herring mass under eutrophic and hypoxic conditions and subsequently how they were replaced by pioneer species that normally live in undisturbed conditions. Species sensitive to low oxygen levels were also given special attention.

Study area
Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur (64<U+00B0>57<U+2032>16<U+2033>N 23<U+00B0>07<U+2032>15<U+2033>W) is a semi-enclosed fjord which opens north to the Brei<U+00F0>afj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur bay in West Iceland (Fig. 1). It has a surface area of ~10 km2, volume 0.2 km3, and maximum depth of 42 m (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018). The tidal range in the fjord is around 4.1 m at spring tide (Mannvit 2011). In 2004, an 830 m long causeway was built across the mouth of the fjord. This structure includes a 230 m long bridge, of which 150 m serves as an effective opening, with the remainder of the causeway blocking water flow. That narrowed the opening of the fjord, but the design is supposed to allow approximately the same exchange of water in the fjord as before the construction, as the opening is situated at the deepest part of the fjord. After the construction, measurements confirmed that the amplitude and timing of the tides are similar inside and outside of the bridge (Mannvit 2011), with the tide inside of Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur delayed by 18 minutes relative to the outside waters. Seawater exchange in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is almost exclusively driven by the tides, with minor effects of e.g. wind and air pressure (J<U+00F3>nsson et al. 2019). There is a strong tidal current of up to at least 7 knots (3.6 m/s) under the bridge between low and high tide (<U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018). The main direction of the current inside Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is south along the west side of the fjord and out toward the bridge along the middle and east coast. The estimated flushing time of the fjord is 2.6 days at spring tide and 6.7 days at neap tide. Freshwater inflow to the fjord is at least two orders of magnitude smaller than the flow under the bridge. Generally, stratification in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is not expected to be common or important, although during calm and cold weather conditions, the freshwater runoff to Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur can create a less dense and colder surface layer, which may facilitate the formation of a thin layer of sea ice and hence limit the ocean-atmosphere exchange of oxygen (J<U+00F3>nsson et al. 2019). Anthropogenic influences on nutrients of the fjord are considered insignificant, as this is a rural area with only one two small dairy farms situated at the fjord. Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is fully saline despite freshwater input from nearby mountains.
Following the mass herring death in the winter 2012/2013, considerable effort was put into research to try to understand the role of different physical parameters in the herring event, including measurements of currents, wind, sea-level, temperature, salinity and oxygen at various positions and depths (see <U+00D3>skarsson et al. 2018 and J<U+00F3>nsson et al. 2019).

Materials and methods 
Sampling and processing
In the year 1999, an environmental impact assessment for a causeway and a bridge across Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur was done by Ing<U+00F3>lfsson (1999) with an extensive sampling campaign of soft bottom fauna in the fjord. A single sample was collected from multiple stations (N = 30) in a grid-like pattern over the fjord using a small Ekman grab (0.0225 m2). Six of the stations used by Ing<U+00F3>lfsson (1999) were selected for this study in 2013, because of their homogenous species composition, relatively high diversity of invertebrates, and similar sediment characteristics, allowing for a fair comparison with sampling in 2013<U+2013>2017. Sampling was conducted in June/July each year. 
The benthic fauna was collected at six stations each year (2013<U+2013>2017) using a Shipek<U+00A9> sediment samplesr (0.04 m<U+00B2> sampling surface; three replicates). The samples were sieved with seawater onboard through a 0.5 mm mesh to retain benthic organisms, which. The retained organisms were immediately preserved in a 5% formalin solution neutralized with borax. Within a week, the samples were transferred to 70% ethanol. An additional sample was collected at each station for sediment analysis and preserved in 70% ethanol. 
At the laboratory, the benthic macrofauna was sorted and identified. Each sSamples wereas dyed with Bengal Rose to facilitate sorting. The macrofauna was identified to species, when possible, otherwise to the lowest possible taxonomic levels. Foraminiferans were not counted or included in the number of taxa. For quality assurance, the same person, the late Gu<U+00F0>mundur V<U+00ED><U+00F0>ir Helgason, verified all annelid identifications in both this study and Ing<U+00F3>lfsson (1999), apart from 2016 when VS and JS oversaw identifications following Helgason<U+2019>s passing in May 2020.
Sediment grain size and organic matter content were assessed at each station, using the Shipek sediment sampler. Small pebbles and shell fragments were separated from the sand and mud fractions by hand. Samples were stored in 70% isopropanol and later dried at 60<U+00B0>C for 24 hours and sieved. To prevent clotting, the samples were stirred 2<U+2013> 3 times during the drying process and gently tapped with a spoon during sieving. The dried samples were sieved using a series of mesh sizes (4000, 2000, 1000, 250, 125, and 63 <U+00B5>m). Each size fraction was then weighed to determine the proportion of sediment in each particle size group. 
The organic matter content of sediment samples was estimated by using loss on ignition (Heiri et al. 2001). Three subsamples of (2 <U+00B1> 0.05 g) were weighed directly in porcelain melting pots and burned at 475<U+00B0>C for 2 hours. Only the fraction <63 <U+00B5>m was used for the analysis of organic content. 

Data analysis 
To assess the diversity of benthic communities, the Shannon-Wiener diversity index (Shannon 1948) was used. This index incorporates species abundance and evenness, providing a detailed measure of community structure. 
The AZTI Marine Biotic Index (AMBI) was utilized to assess the ecological quality of the benthic habitats in the sampling stations in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur by classifying species into ecological groups according to their sensitivity to pollution and disturbance (Borja et al. 2000). AMBI evaluates the impact of environmental stress on species composition and is widely used in benthic ecological studies. 
The Norwegian Quality Index (NQI1), a multimetric index that combines the Shannon-Wiener index with AMBI, was applied providing sensitivity to both disturbance and biodiversity changes (Rygg 2006). 
A normalized version of the indices (nShannon, nAMBI and nNQI1) was calculated, expressing results as Ecological Quality Ratios (EQRs) ranging from 0 to 1, following the procedure outlined in Borja et al. (2007). This normalization enables standardized comparisons across indices, ecosystems, and time periods. The indices were calculated using the BBI package (Cordier et al. 2021) in R (R Core Team, 2023), ensuring a consistent and reliable assessment of community recovery and ecological quality throughout the study. 
We analysed benthic community composition data using standardized, untransformed count data across 104 harmonized taxonomic categories. Using the vegan package in R (Oksanen et al. 2024) we employed complementary multivariate techniques: Permutational Multivariate Analysis of Variance (PERMANOVA), and Non-metric Multidimensional Scaling (NMDS).
PERMANOVA tested for significant differences in community composition across space and time using the 'adonis2' function with 999 permutations and Bray-Curtis dissimilarity. The model included station and year as factors. NMDS visualized community composition patterns using the 'metaMDS' function with Bray-Curtis dissimilarities. Species contributing <U+2265> 1% to total abundance were included in the analysis. Environmental vectors were fitted to the ordination using the 'envfit' function, and only vectors with p < 0.05 were retained. Station trajectories were visualized using arrows connecting consecutive years, with arrow length representing the magnitude of community change. 
A principal components analysis (PCA) and associated biplots were used to analyse species composition among years and to compare stations within years. 
To analyse the temporal and spatial patterns in sediment grain size distribution, we employed beta regression models using the betareg package in R (Cribari-Neto & Zeileis, 2010). Beta regression is appropriate for analysing proportional data that are bounded between 0 and 1. Separate models were fitted for each grain size category (20 <U+03BC>m, 63 <U+03BC>m, 125 <U+03BC>m, 250 <U+03BC>m, and 1000 <U+03BC>m), with sampling year and station as predictors. 
Due to non-normal distribution of organic content data, we employed the non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test to examine temporal differences across the five-year study period. This approach is robust against violations of normality and appropriate for comparing multiple independent groups with continuous response variables. Following significant Kruskal-Wallis results, Dunn's test with Bonferroni correction was used for post-hoc pairwise comparisons between years and stations.
To assess temporal shifts in community adaptation to hydrodynamic conditions, we calculated dynamic adaptation scores for each station across sampling years (1999 and 2013<U+2013>2017). These scores (taken from Clare et al. 2022) were derived from weighted combinations of key functional traits: tube-dwelling ability (30%), surface deposit feeding (20%), planktonic larval development (20%), and free-living mobility (30%). The scores were weighted by species' adjusted densities to reflect community-level adaptations, allowing us to track changes in the functional composition of benthic assemblages over time. This approach enabled quantification of how communities' capacity to cope with dynamic sedimentary conditions evolved throughout the study period, particularly in response to environmental perturbations.

Community structure
Non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) ordination revealed clear temporal patterns in benthic community composition from 2013 to 2017 (stress = 0.138). The analysis, based on Bray-Curtis dissimilarities of 104 harmonized taxonomic categories across 36 samples (6 stations <U+00D7> 6 years), showed distinct recovery trajectories for different stations. Environmental fitting identified significant correlations with sediment characteristics (p < 0.05): organic content (r<U+00B2> = 0.640), fine mud <63<U+03BC>m (r<U+00B2> = 0.536), very fine particles <20<U+03BC>m (r<U+00B2> = 0.469), and medium-fine particles <125<U+03BC>m (r<U+00B2> = 0.334). 
The analysis revealed distinct patterns in station trajectories and community dynamics over the years (Fig. 3). Station A7 exhibited the longest recovery path, while C4, in the deepest part of the fjord, had the shortest. Initially, the animal communities at all stations moved in similar directions, with E3 and E4 showing slightly higher angles. The community's movement was most pronounced in 2013, gradually decreasing in subsequent years. The NDMS analysis highlighted that 2013 and 2014 were distinct, clustering by year rather than station. In contrast, data from 2015 to 2017 showed more station-based grouping, with E3 and E4 diverging from the others. Over time, the later years (2015<U+2013>2017) began to resemble 1999 more closely than the earlier years, indicating a shift towards pre-impact conditions.

The NMDS ordination (Fig. 3) showed clear spatial segregation of dominant species. C. capitata, the most abundant species (27.0% of total abundance), was strongly isolated in positive NMDS1 space (NMDS1 = 1.91), distinctly separated from all other species. A group of five species/taxa, including Polydora sp. (10.4%) and Spio sp. (7.1%), occupied neutral positions in the ordination space, suggesting their generalist distribution patterns. Species associated with positive NMDS2 scores included Ophelina. acuminata (6.5%) and Macoma. calcarea (5.4%), while a distinct cluster of species including Tubificoides benedii (3.3%) and S. inflatum (3.3%) showed strong negative NMDS1 scores, suggesting their association with later recovery stages of the community succession. 

Temporal Consistency and Environmental Relationships 
Theis recovery process showed spatial and temporal consistency across Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur, with community composition changing over time during the recovery period between 2013 and 2017 (NMDS temporal gradient: r<U+00B2> = 0.702, p = 0.001). Tests of multivariate dispersion revealed no significant differences in community variability either between years (F = 0.719, p = 0.587) or between stations (F = 0.569, p = 0.723), indicating an orderly, fjord-wide recovery rather than patchy recolonization. This structured succession was strongly linked to the processing or dispersion of organic matter (r<U+00B2> = 0.549, p = 0.001), suggesting that the decomposition of herring biomass drove a coordinated ecosystem recovery. 
The distance between the 2017 communities and the 1999 state (0.657 <U+00B1> 0.271 units in NMDS space) exceeded the final year-to-year changes during recovery (0.314 <U+00B1> 0.191 units), indicating that while the community had stabilized by 2017, it had shifted to a slightly? different configuration than its pre-disturbance state.

Discussion
The mass-death of the herring event in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur in 2013 provided an excellent opportunity to study the effects of a single-event hypoxia and extensive organic enrichment eutrophication on a fully marine benthic marine ecosystem. Immediately following the 2013 event, the number of species declined sharply to an all-time low of 21 taxa, compared to 57 taxa in 1999 and 69 taxa in 1999. 
Timeline of recovery
The 1999 community structure was that of a stable, mature benthic ecosystem shaped primarily by physical factors such as sediment stability and hydrodynamics. Distinct spatial patterns reflected localized hydrodynamic and sedimentary regimes, where the western stations (E3, E4) supported opportunistic species adapted to dynamic, high-energy environments, including Polydora sp. and Spio sp. With traits such as tSpecies with tube-dwelling, surface deposit feeding, and planktonic larval dispersal allowed these species toseemed to thrive in physically disturbed sediments at stations E3 and E4. Conversely, the eastern and southern Sstations (A7, B5, B8, C4) exhibited greater functional diversity and resource partitioning, where species such as Macoma calcarea, Scalibregma inflatum, and Ophelina acuminata utilized deeper sediments through suspension feeding, bioturbation, and vertical resource partitioning.
The immediate aftermath of the herring event erased the spatial community patterns observed in 1999. By 2013, the polychaete Capitella capitata dominated nearly all stations, resulting in low species diversity and functional homogenization. The dominance of C. capitata in 2013, which was not found in 1999, reflects its exceptional adaptability, relying on surface and subsurface deposit feeding, high tolerance for organic enrichment, and lecithotrophic larval production. These traits facilitated its rapid colonization and initial dominance following the herring event. 
The ecosystem recovery was marked by significant shifts in feeding guild composition. In 2013, deposit feeders such as C. capitata dominated, comprising 75% of the community. Exploiting the nutrient-laden environment created by the disturbance, C. capitata persisted into 2014, dominating at 4 of the 6 stations. Their role in breaking down the organic-rich herring mass must have facilitated the recovery of the ecosystem. By 2015, its densities dropped significantly, and the species had completely vanished from samples by 2017. This temporary overshoot of C. capitata aligns with patterns observed in other marine ecosystems recovering from hypoxia (Beukema 1999; Silva et al. 2017).
The rise and fall of C. capitata also was the driver for sudden emergence and subsequent demise of epibenthic predators in 2014. By 2014<U+2013>2017, an increase in deposit/suspension feeders (e.g. M. calcarea, and S. inflatum) and predators (e.g. Eteone longa, and Harmothoe extenuata) reflected a transition toward a more stable trophic structure. This suggests that predator-prey interactions, particularly the role of predators in reducing the density of C. capitata, were crucial in intermediate stages facilitating ecosystem recovery (Diaz and& Rosenberg 1995).
While C. capitata remained prominent in 2014, other species such as Spio sp., Tubificoides kozloffi, and Chaetozone setosa began increasing in density. Predator species, including E. longa, also showed notable increases, suggesting a trophic response to the abundance of C. capitata and other pioneer species. The gradual increase in species diversity during this phase marks the beginning of functional reorganization (Silva et al. 2017; Ghanavati Asl et al. 2023).
The year 2015 represented an intermediate phase in the recovery trajectory. C. capitata densities dropped significantly. Key transitional species such as O. acuminata, Mediomastus fragilis, and M. calcarea became prominent across stations. The year 2015 showed greater spatial variability, with distinct groupings emerging. The western stations (E3, E4) began to support species resembling their 1999 composition, including Polydora sp. and Tubificoides benedii. Eastern and southern stations supported species characteristic of stable sediment environments, such as S. inflatum and C. setosa.
Between 2016 and 2017, the benthic community demonstrated substantial recovery, with species composition resembling pre-disturbance patterns. The western stations (E3, E4) were once again dominated by Polydora sp., while other stations supported a diverse mix of equilibrium species, including Scoloplos armiger, M. calcarea, and Mediomastus fragilis. By 2017, species diversity had exceeded 1999 levels, and the disappearance of C. capitata signified a completed shift toward relative ecosystem stabilization. This recovery trajectory aligns with established models of benthic community succession following hypoxia (Diaz and& Rosenberg 1995; Silva et al. 2017; Liu et al. 2024).
Molluscs and arthropods often are wiped out during hypoxic events, as these groups are dependent on oxygen-rich waters (Raman et al. 2015). In 1999, there were 15 species of molluscs (12 bivalve and 3 gastropod species), and 7 species of arthropods present in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur. In 2013, only 2 of each group were found on two stations each and in low densities; the Arthropods were Acari and Crassicorophium bonellii and the molluscs Arctica islandica and Polyplacophora (Tonicellia sp). The number of mollusc species increased to 9 in 2014 (8 species of bivalves and the gastropod Onoba aculeus) and the number of arthropods to four (Caprella septentrionalis, Protomedeia fasciata, Balanus sp, and Verruca stroemia). By 2017, the numbers had reached or passed the number of species in 1999, i.e. 20 for molluscs (12 bivalves and 8 gGastropod species) and 7 for aArthropods. These developments among molluscs and arthropods rhyme well with our community analyses and overall changes in species composition, from anoxic to natural conditions. 

Sediment dynamics and environmental drivers
Sediment analysis revealed distinct spatial patterns across the fjord, with 1) the southern stations (A7, B5, B8) were characterized by coarser sediments, including mixtures of sand and gravel, whereas the sediments at the western stations (E3, E4) were richer in finer material and exhibited pronounced temporal changes, likely influenced by tidal currents and localized organic matter deposition (Komendi<U+0107> et al. 2024), which in our case may have followinged the herring event (Komendi<U+0107> et al. 2024). Station C4 was located between the two groups and was also the most variable in sediment composition. Station A7 was the last to show signs of ecosystem recovery but also the most variable in organic matter content. Overall, organic matter content peaked in 2013 due to herring decomposition but declined sharply thereafter, stabilizing by 2014. The localized sediment dynamics at E3 and E4 further underscore the role of hydrodynamics in shaping recovery trajectories.
While By 2017, the benthic community largely resembled its 1999 state by 2017, but differences remained. NMDS analysis showed a measurable distance between the 2017 community and the 1999 baseline community (0.657 <U+00B1> 0.271 units), indicating a shift to a slightly altered stable state. This transformation may reflect lingering impacts of the herring event, natural variability in sediment conditions, or long-term succession processes. Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur's recovery compared to similar ecosystems highlights the importance of its pristine baseline condition, limited anthropogenic impacts, healthy source populations in the vicinity, and favourable hydrodynamic conditions. These factors likely contributed to the system's resilience and ability to recover within five years (Borja et al. 2010; Beukema et al. 1999).
Benthic community structure can change vastly over two decades, without catastrophic events but due to changes in sediment structure, possibly driven by storms (Komendi<U+0107> et al. 2024). We have no sediment or organic matter data for 1999, but sediment structure was constant similar at 4 of 6 stations from 2013-<U+2013>2017, with some annual changes occurring at stations E3 and E4, which also showed distinct species compositions in 1999, and 2015-<U+2013> 2017. Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is sheltered from storms but is probably occasionally affected by them occasionally. However, a constant factor is the tidal activity, which may have greater effects on sediment and community structure at stations E3 and E4, which also is the locations where the dead herring mass was swept towards in the wake of the herring event<U+2019>s wake. 
Importantly, Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur is a fjord with a narrow outflow and not exposed to much human activity (for example, there is no ship traffic or aquaculture). While the herring event was caused partly by calm weather, the area but still faces its share of storms and tidal activity and combinations of those, including the one that lead to the herring event. This system is more pristine than most studied for organic pollutions, where the influx of nutrients is generally is related to human activity. However, changes in sediment structure occurred at varying rates among our sampling stations, and we attribute the annual variation at stations E3 and E4 (and to a lesser extent C4) to varying depth and its interactions with tidal activity, which is greater in the eastern western part of the fjord relative to that to the east or to southern part of the fjord (stations B5, A7 and B8).<U+00A0>

The importance of local biodiversity in the recovery process
The rapid recovery observed in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur suggests that local biodiversity contributed to resilience through at least two complementary mechanisms. First, recovery appears to have been facilitated by the presence within the local species pool of a species able to exploitcapable of exploiting the extreme post-disturbance conditions. Second, recovery in Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur was likely accelerated by the existence of healthy and species-rich benthic communities immediately outside the fjord. Once the extreme organic load diminished, recolonisation could occur from nearby source populations rather than relying on slow immigration from distant areas. In benthic systems, recolonisation commonly depends on the availability of recruits from nearby undisturbed areas, and recovery is typically faster where disturbance leaves adjacent source populations intact (Stephenson et al. 2019). Taken together, these two mechanisms fit well with the insurance hypothesis of biodiversity (Yachi and Loreau 1999; Loreau et al. 2021). In this framework, high biodiversity provides <U+201C>insurance<U+201D> because it increases the probability that some species will persist, expand, or compensate functionally when environmental conditions change abruptly. In Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur, biodiversity may therefore have operated both as functional insurance within the disturbed system, through the rapid expansion of an opportunistic deposit-feeder capable of exploiting and helping process the organic load, and as spatial insurance at the landscape scale, through the proximity of intact source communities capable of repopulating the fjord as conditions improved. The recovery observed here was therefore not simply a return of species numbers, but a recovery process supported by both functional redundancy and spatial connectivity.
Although our study did not directly test the effects of formal protection directly, the results underlines that species-rich, pristine surrounding habitats can enhance resilience and accelerate recovery after severe disturbance by acting as reservoirs of biodiversity and propagule supply (Stephenson et al. 2019). This highlights the importance of marine protected areas and habitat networks in conserving biodiversity, maintaining connectivity, and supporting recovery in adjacent disturbed areas.
Thus, Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur's fast recovery compared to similar ecosystems highlights the importance of its pristine condition, limited anthropogenic impacts, healthy source populations in the vicinity, and favourable hydrodynamic conditions. These factors likely all contributed to the system's resilience and ability to recover within five years (Borja et al. 2010; Beukema et al. 1999).

Conclusion 
The recovery of Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur's benthic community following the 2013 hypoxic and massive organic enrichment event provides valuable insights into ecosystem resilience and succession dynamics. The initial dominance of opportunistic species like C. capitata was followed by the gradual re-emergence of equilibrium species and functional diversity, ultimately leading to ecosystem restabilization in 3-<U+2013>4 years.
While the community approached its pre-disturbance state by 2017, subtle differences highlight the complexity of recovery processes and the potential for alternative stable states. Long-term monitoring remains essential to fully understand the interplay between episodic disturbances, natural variability, and benthic ecosystem resilience. Generally, studies like these span few years but baseline data, representing natural/pristine conditions, comprised of several years are generally lacking. 
This study underscores the importance of localized environmental conditions, such as biodiversity, hydrodynamics and sediment stability, in shaping recovery outcomes. Kolgrafafj<U+00F6>r<U+00F0>ur's recovery can serve as a future model for understanding and managing other marine systems impacted by hypoxia and organic enrichment.
